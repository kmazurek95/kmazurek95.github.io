01

Research Narrative

The thread connecting every project

Every project I build asks a version of the same question: how do contextual environments shape individual political outcomes? The method is always multilevel — individuals nested in contexts, units nested in groups, observations nested in structures. The data changes. The domain changes. The core analytical logic does not.

My thesis built an NLP pipeline to classify interest group prominence across 78,000 congressional floor speeches, asking how organized interests shape the informational environment of legislative debate. My public attitudes project modeled how neighborhood inequality affects individual redistribution preferences across 1,500 Dutch neighborhoods. My election analysis modeled the 2020-2024 county-level partisan swing across 3,152 US counties, finding that 31% of swing variance sits at the state level — a structural finding about where contextual effects actually live.

This is not just a methods story. It is a substantive claim: political behavior cannot be fully explained by individual characteristics alone. Context matters, and you need the right statistical architecture to model it. Whether the context is a congressional chamber, a neighborhood, or a state media market, the analytical challenge is the same — and so is the toolkit I bring to it.

I also bring measurement methodology thinking to AI evaluation. My published article "What Political Science Can Teach AI Evaluation" connects construct validity, inter-rater reliability, and sampling bias from social science to the challenges facing AI evaluation pipelines today. The same rigor I apply to operationalizing "interest group prominence" applies to designing rubrics for model output quality.
