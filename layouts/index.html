01

Research Program

From institutional context to distributional politics

One question runs through everything I have built: when structural economic forces create winners and losers, what determines whether societies respond with solidarity or exclusion? I started asking this about welfare states, inequality, and political attitudes. Now I ask it about AI.

The empirical foundation comes from four completed projects spanning congressional speech, Dutch neighborhoods, European welfare states, and US county-level elections. The finding that keeps showing up: institutional context outperforms individual and local conditions at every scale I have tested. Neighborhood inequality explains only 3.4% of redistribution attitude variance, but country-level institutions explain 7.7%. In the 2024 election, 31% of county-level partisan swing sits at the state level. The consistency of this pattern across different countries, scales, and data sources is what brought me to AI.

If the distributional consequences of artificial intelligence depend on institutional configurations, then neither techno-optimism nor techno-pessimism captures what is actually happening. The question is which institutions, protecting whom, under what conditions. AI does not create one kind of loser. It creates different kinds of losers through different channels: labor market displacement, energy costs socialized through utility bills, geopolitical dependencies on critical mineral supply chains. These channels interact. They often land on the same populations.

My position in this landscape is unusual. I work in applied AI evaluation, training and assessing the same models whose societal consequences I study. I have written benchmark questions, designed evaluation rubrics, and spent years labeling outputs at scale. That experience shapes how I think about measurement, about what AI can and cannot do, and about the gap between how the AI industry talks about progress and what that progress actually means for the people building it.
