The current debate over the economic impact of artificial intelligence (AI) has become routine. The question most often asked is: how quickly will AI replace jobs? Which jobs will AI replace first? In late February, Citrini Research released a stress test modeling a potential self-reinforcing process of labor displacement. Following the release of this report, Citadel Securities responded with a position paper stating that technology creates additional space for consumption. While the debate continues, there is another area that deserves equal consideration.

AI is increasing energy costs. This trend can be quantified, exhibits regressive properties, and is increasing. The reason why it occurs is due to specific regulatory processes that transfer the costs of building out the necessary infrastructure to meet the demands from these new users onto consumers that largely won't capture any of the benefits. These two trends are compounding each other, because both lead to higher costs for the same population groups.

The energy trend has received far too little press. The evidence supporting this trend is substantial.

## The Grid

Data centers are the physical infrastructure of the emerging economy of AI. All queries, images produced using generative models, and workflows executed by agents require processing time on graphics processing units (GPUs) that consume power drawn from the electrical grid. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), global data center electricity usage reached approximately 415 terawatt-hours in 2024, accounting for 1.5 percent of all electricity generated globally (IEA, Energy and AI, 2025). According to the IEA's base-case forecast, this number will double by 2030, equating to Japan's total electricity consumption.

## U.S. Concentration

American data centers alone accounted for an estimated 183 terawatt hours in 2024. That number is expected to reach 426 terawatt hours by 2030 (IEA, 2025, base case). Therefore, by the end of this decade, data centers are projected to represent some of the largest individual categories of electricity consumers in the United States, exceeding the combined output of traditional heavy industries.

The method used to distribute costs is not designed to allocate costs based on who generates the demand. When a hyperscale company such as Amazon Web Services or Microsoft Azure builds a 300-megawatt campus within a utility's service territory, the local grid requires upgrades, including new transmission lines, substations, and generation capacity. Under current federal regulations and state public utility commission regulations, these capital expenditures are allocated across all ratepayers via base rate increases. The data center itself pays only for volumetric electricity usage.

PJM Interconnection, a regional grid serving roughly 65 million people across 13 states and the District of Columbia, has seen dramatic price increases across recent capacity auctions, with some zones experiencing nearly an elevenfold increase in capacity auction prices between the 2024/25 and 2026/27 delivery periods. In its annual report on the PJM markets published in March 2026, Monitoring Analytics stated that data center load growth resulted in a cumulative increase in system-wide costs of $23.1 billion during the last three capacity auctions. As reported by CNBC (December 3, 2025), Monitoring Analytics earlier characterized this dynamic as a "massive wealth transfer" from consumers to the data center industry. A Bloomberg analysis examining wholesale electricity pricing across tens of thousands of U.S. locations found that wholesale prices in areas surrounding large concentrations of data centers had risen by as much as 267 percent compared to five years earlier (Bloomberg, September 2025). Consumer Reports also published a story documenting a Virginia homeowner in Manassas, VA, who saw his monthly electricity bill jump from approximately $100 to $281 in January 2026 (Consumer Reports, March 2026).

According to a report issued by the Brookings Institution, nationwide U.S. electricity costs have increased by 42% since 2019, while overall inflation rates have increased by 29% (Brookings, "Confronting and Addressing Rising Energy Bills Linked to Data Centers," March 2026). Other estimates provide slightly smaller gaps. EESI cites a 27% increase in average retail rates through year-end 2025 using EIA data (EESI, February 2026), and CEPR cites IEA data showing U.S. household electricity prices were 32% higher than in 2019 (CEPR, March 2026). Differences exist because various studies use different measures of electricity costs: total dollar amounts charged for electricity on a customer's bill versus averages per kilowatt-hour of retail electricity rates. Regardless of methodology employed, all studies indicate that electricity costs are growing faster than general inflation; the gap between electricity costs and general inflation is growing larger.

While data centers are not the only contributing factor, a Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory and Brattle Group analysis covering the five years ending in 2024 found that data center loads were not the primary driver of rate increases over that earlier window (LBNL/Brattle, Recent Retail Electricity Price Trends, October 2025). Factors contributing to increasing rates include aging grid infrastructure, post-wildfire hardening requirements, and equipment supply chain issues. The Economist recently made a version of this argument, pointing out that big tech firms are investing in their own generation capacity, including Microsoft's Three Mile Island restart, Alphabet's $4.75 billion acquisition of Intersect Power, and Meta's nuclear startup investments, and negotiating flexible tariff arrangements that let utilities monetize spare capacity without expensive expansion (The Economist, "Americans' Electricity Bills Are Up. Don't Blame AI", March 2026).

This captures something real about how the largest hyperscalers are behaving, and it is consistent with the LBNL/Brattle finding for the period through 2024. But it understates two things. The supply additions are years away from coming online; the Three Mile Island restart is targeted for 2027 at the earliest, and new nuclear projects routinely slip. The bills going up now cannot be offset by capacity that does not yet exist. And the flexibility arrangements apply to a small subset of the largest, most sophisticated operators. They do not apply to the long tail of midsize and colocation facilities that make up most of the load growth, and they do not change the regulatory rules that socialize transmission and substation upgrade costs across all ratepayers. Data centers are now the fastest-growing category of electricity demand, and Goldman Sachs forecasts that they will generate nearly half of future U.S. demand growth (Goldman Sachs Research, 2026). The way upgrade costs are distributed ensures that those costs will be borne by residential customers regardless of whether those customers generated the original demand.

## Regressiveness of Increased Costs

The regressiveness inherent in this cost increase stems from the fact that a $25/month increase in electricity cost affects a household earning $40k/year differently than it does one earning $150k/year. Geographically speaking, data centers tend to locate where land is inexpensive and grid capacity exists, which includes suburban, exurban and rural areas. Conversely, the financial returns from AI accrue entirely to shareholders and high-skilled employees concentrated in select metropolitan areas. There is essentially no overlap between the populations bearing the burden of funding for data center-related infrastructure and those receiving the benefits from increased value creation attributed to AI.

## Political Implications

Researchers have begun measuring politically relevant implications stemming from this phenomenon. Voeten (2025) investigated a Dutch policy aimed at increasing household natural-gas taxes. Renters affected by this policy demonstrated an increase in voting propensity for radical-right parties ranging from 5 to 6 percentage points. The basis for this behavior was economic; affected voters did not alter their views regarding immigration or European Union membership. Instead, affected voters grew angry about energy costs and punished those parties they believed responsible for increasing those costs (Comparative Political Studies, 2025).

Sweden-based research found similar results. Brännlund and Peterson (2024) showed that rising electricity prices in Sweden translated into higher voter support for far-right parties, with the radical right vote share concentrated in electorates where households faced the highest electricity bills relative to disposable income. Niskanen and Anshelm (2026) document the institutional consequences. Since the Sweden Democrats entered parliament in 2010, the party has made resistance to wind power central to its identity politics, intercepting local resistance discourses and reframing them as a defense of rural Sweden against urban elites. By the 2022 election, this strategy had driven the conservative-liberal bloc into a coalition with the Sweden Democrats that reversed Sweden's renewable energy strategy and shifted state support from wind toward nuclear power. The mechanism Brännlund and Peterson identify at the voter level became, over fifteen years, a parliamentary majority.

Finally, Prakash, Awaworyi Churchill, and Smyth (2025) demonstrate that energy-poor households in Australia are 8 percentage points less likely to support either of the two major parties (Labor or the Liberal-National Coalition) than to support a minor party or independent, and 1.4 times more likely to vote for right-wing populist minor parties (One Nation and the United Australia Party/Trumpet of Patriots) than for other minor parties or independents. In the two-party preferred comparison, energy poverty reduces support for Labor by 3.7 percentage points relative to the Coalition. The pattern is consistent with voters associating Labor's stronger climate policies with higher energy costs, while the Coalition's more fossil-fuel-aligned position resonates with energy-stressed households.

Consumer Reports conducted a survey of 2,146 U.S. adults resulting in an estimate that 78 percent expressed some level of concern about their electricity costs increasing due to new data center construction in their vicinity (Consumer Reports, November 2025). Whether those cost increases result in voter dissatisfaction with existing parties, employing similar political mechanisms to those demonstrated in the Netherlands, Sweden, and Australia, remains an open empirical question. Nonetheless, structural elements remain: regressive cost allocation, geographical concentration of cost burdens, and highly visible concentration of economic benefits derived from AI.

## The Checkbook

The labor displacement debate is still unresolved. But the early evidence points in a direction that compounds the energy story.

Hui, Reshef, and Zhou (2024) discovered that freelancers in AI-exposed occupations saw reductions in both employment and earnings after ChatGPT was released. The reduction in employment and earnings was greatest among experienced, high-earning workers (Organization Science, 2024). Henseke et al. (2025) developed a task-based Generative AI Susceptibility Index and determined that U.K. job vacancies in June 2025 were approximately 5.5% below where the pre-ChatGPT relationship between AI exposure and hiring would have predicted them (arXiv:2507.22748, 2025). Using monthly occupation-level unemployment data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics' Local Area Unemployment Statistics (LAUS), Frank, Ahn, and Moro (2025) found that an ensemble of AI exposure models accounted for an additional 18% of variation in unemployment risk across occupations, states, and time.

But there is real counterevidence. Using a multi-country, multi-sector macroeconomic model, Freire (2024) found that "AI results in only small changes at the aggregated level of GDP and employment," alongside significant increases in income inequality and major sectoral shifts due to AI (Structural Change and Economic Dynamics, 2024). Citadel's response to Citrini's scenario cited historical data to show that technology generally expands the consumption frontier rather than causing permanent worker dislocation. And Masoud (2024) used dynamic panel data from ten high-income economies (2015–2023) to determine that the impact of AI on unemployment is statistically insignificant (Technological Sustainability, 2024).

Honestly, we don't know how fast it will happen. We do know the direction. Generative AI targets non-routine cognitive tasks: software development, legal research, financial analysis, and content creation. These were the jobs that displaced manufacturing workers were expected to use to rebuild their careers. Using a systems-dynamics model with Australian data, Occhipinti et al. (2025) identified a tipping point beyond which "even high rates of new job creation cannot prevent declines in consumption," finding that a 10.8-fold increase in new job creation rates would be required to offset declines in disposable income (Scientific Reports, 2025). How far off this tipping point is remains an open question.

For the purposes of this argument, the exact answer to the labor-displacement debate is less important than the fact that downward pressure on wages and employment in AI-exposed occupations can be seen in the data. That downward pressure on wages and employment does not have to result in widespread unemployment to compound the energy-cost story. All it has to do is produce sufficient income uncertainty in sufficient numbers of households so they can't afford the subsequent increases in electricity prices.

## The Compound

These two channels aren't independent. They intersect.

Lower-income families and exurban areas are the ones who bear most of the brunt of data center grid costs. Middle-class knowledge workers, whose purchasing power drives much of consumer spending, increasingly find themselves among those facing reduced incomes due to AI-related wage compression. In many cases, households struggling with increasing energy costs are the same households experiencing income insecurity.

From a household perspective, these are not two separate issues. They are viewed as a single economic contraction: costs increasing, income uncertain. While researchers can identify the analytical separation between them, individuals experiencing them live through them as a single issue.

Research on energy costs and voting behaviors indicates what occurs as this contraction develops. The mechanism proceeds through perceived economic injustice, not ideology. Research testing whether voters support candidates when costs increase while identifiable corporations earn extraordinary profits exists for energy-transition policies (Brännlund and Peterson 2024; Voeten 2025; Prakash et al. 2025). However, research testing the effect of data-center-driven electric costs and increased labor-market pressures caused by AI has yet to occur. Furthermore, research testing these factors together has also not yet occurred. This gap in research represents an area where future research should take place.

Institutionally speaking, this situation makes closing the gap difficult. Energy policy resides within multiple different government agencies. AI policy resides within other government agencies. Labor policy resides within yet another group of agencies. Those responsible for approving permits for locating data centers have no authority to consider worker displacement. Those responsible for implementing programs supporting workforce skills resulting from AI innovations have no access to information regarding local utility rate cases. Welfare-state instruments established to protect against economic shock waves such as energy-assistance programs, unemployment-insurance programs, and progressive-tariff structures were all established under a paradigm where energy costs and technological displacement were separate issues occurring in different demographics at different times.

Zimmermann (2023) argues in European Political Science that "the pathway of such green transitions is closely interrelated with welfare setups." However, Zimmermann does not include AI in her conceptualization. Vona (2023) argued in Ecological Economics that the path toward a "just transition" appears narrower than originally thought once distributional effects are considered simultaneously across multiple dimensions. However, Vona did not address AI specifically. The dynamic Voeten documents has been shown for natural gas taxes but has not been applied to costs attributable to data centers. The compounding of energy burden and labor displacement on similar demographic groups remains unaddressed within the welfare-state literature.

The AI economy is producing value. The only remaining issue is how the costs of creating that value are distributed. Based upon the evidence provided above, it is apparent that the distribution is regressive and operates through two compounded pathways affecting demographic groups with little ability to influence public policy choices. Whether democracies choose to deal with the issue intentionally or merely allow it to happen through default is currently a policy decision being made by inaction today.
