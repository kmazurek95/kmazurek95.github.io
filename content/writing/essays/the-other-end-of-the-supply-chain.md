In December 2025, Noble Helium began exploration drilling at Kinambo village in Tanzania's Rukwa region. The company had spent the previous year completing preliminary surveys, compensating local residents for land access, and setting up the informal economy that attaches itself to any exploration camp: 450 jobs in security, food procurement, distribution of goods and lubricants, road improvements. In a December interview with the *Daily News* of Tanzania, Noble's country representative, Pius Mwita, said the new drilling program would add another 50 positions for young people and urged them to come forward in large numbers.

Two months later, Iranian missiles struck Qatar's Ras Laffan facility and took roughly a quarter to a third of global helium supply offline (Qatar accounts for an estimated 25–32% of global helium production, depending on the source). Helium spot prices doubled within weeks. The companies drilling in Rukwa, along with Pulsar Helium in northern Minnesota and emerging primary helium plays in Canada and South Africa, went from speculative investments to strategic imperatives overnight.

The discourse around the helium shock has been almost entirely about the top of the supply chain. Which fabs run out first. Whether Washington should reclassify helium as a critical mineral. Which industrial gas majors benefit from pricing power. What this means for Samsung, SK Hynix, NVIDIA. This is the register in which AI infrastructure is normally discussed: the customer, the hyperscaler, the chip.

The bottom of the supply chain is in Rukwa. And Solomon Islands. And the Atacama, and Katanga, and the Great Artesian Basin. The communities that will host the material production for the next phase of the AI buildout are already inside a sequence that has played out before, for cobalt and lithium and rare earths. The academic literature on extractive consultation and consent has documented that sequence with considerable precision. AI infrastructure discourse is proceeding as if none of it exists.

This post extends an argument I have been developing about AI infrastructure as a missing variable in energy security analysis ([Mazurek 2026, working paper](/writing/academic/typology-paper/)). The standard treatment of critical mineral convergence focuses on cobalt, lithium, and rare earths. The Hormuz shock has made clear that the convergence extends to critical gases, and that the political economy of extraction at the bottom of the AI supply chain deserves the same analytical weight as the geopolitics of supply at the top.

## What is happening in Rukwa

The Rukwa Basin is in southwestern Tanzania, along the East African Rift system. It is geologically unusual. Most of the world's helium comes as a byproduct of natural gas production, which means helium supply is economically subordinate to LNG economics. When QatarEnergy shut down LNG at Ras Laffan in early March 2026, helium production stopped regardless of helium demand. Rukwa is different. The rifting of the Archean Tanzanian craton has produced what geologists call a primary helium system: helium is the economic driver, not a byproduct. Durham University researchers working with Helium One have estimated Rukwa's prospective recoverable resources at roughly 138 billion cubic feet of helium, a figure the company describes as a substantial fraction of current global reserves.

This has attracted three publicly listed companies. Helium One Global, AIM-listed in London, has been working Rukwa since 2015 and announced a helium discovery at its Itumbula West-1 well in 2024, with flow rates reaching 5.5 percent helium continuously and peak measurements of 7.6 percent. In March 2025, the company accepted a formal offer for a mining license covering 480 square kilometers of the basin. Noble Helium, the Australian company at Kinambo village, is at an earlier exploration stage but has a partnership with the University of Dar es Salaam's School of Mines to train Tanzania's first cohort of helium geophysicists. Rift Helium is planning an AIM IPO in March 2026, with Pulsar Helium's management team as founding shareholders, explicitly positioning itself as a "third-mover" in the basin.

The development story around these projects is framed, in Tanzanian media and in investor presentations, in terms of national benefit. Helium One has spent tens of millions of shillings on rehabilitating schools in Itumbula, Mpapa, Mkulwe, and Ivuna wards, and on completing the Mkoko Dispensary. Tanzanian mining regulations typically require corporate social responsibility spending only after production begins; Helium One has done it during exploration, which is unusually proactive. The government has granted a Certificate of Incentives for mineral processing. The framing is a virtuous cycle: global demand drives investment, investment drives local infrastructure, infrastructure drives development.

The frame is not wrong. It is also not the whole story. The extractives literature is the record of what tends to happen next.

## What the literature predicts

The academic scholarship on extractive industry consultation and consent is mature, cross-regional, and depressingly consistent. Flemmer and Schilling-Vacaflor, writing on the first state-led consultation processes in Bolivia's and Peru's hydrocarbon sectors, identified three mechanisms that systematically limit effective indigenous participation: an absence of community ownership of the consultation process itself, difficulties among indigenous groups in articulating their own demands against asymmetric corporate resources, and outcomes that are limited or very general (Flemmer and Schilling-Vacaflor, "Unfulfilled promises of the consultation approach," *Third World Quarterly*, 2016). The framework of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent, enshrined in international law since the 2007 UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, has been implemented in ways that Klein and colleagues have described as treating consultation as a one-off reputational exercise rather than an ongoing relationship.

Ash's 2024 study of nickel exploration in Solomon Islands is the freshest piece of evidence on this, and the most directly relevant because Rukwa is at the exploration stage. Ash documented that mineral exploration, before any mine is built, before any production begins, generated more negative than positive impacts for affected communities. The timeline is critical. Exploration is the period when the local economy is reorganizing around the project, when land access is being negotiated, when the community's relationship to its own territory is being redefined by a process that most participants do not understand as well as the company does. By the time production begins and the bigger political questions arrive, the institutional patterns are already set.

Jerez and colleagues, writing on lithium extraction in Chile's Salar de Atacama, developed the analytical frame most directly relevant to the AI infrastructure case (Jerez, Garcés, and Torres, "Lithium extractivism and water injustices in the Salar de Atacama, Chile," *Political Geography*, 2021). They describe "green extractivism" as a pattern in which demand vectors framed as climate-positive at the point of consumption produce dispossession at the point of extraction. The lithium in a European electric vehicle is the same lithium whose brine mining depletes the aquifers of Atacameño communities. Jerez's formulation of "the colonial shadow of green electromobility" captures the geographic asymmetry: the benefits accrue in the Global North, the costs accrue in places where the political and legal infrastructure to contest those costs is weaker.

The helium in an NVIDIA Blackwell chip cools the lithography optics that etch the circuits that run the AI models that are reshaping the labor markets of San Francisco, Seoul, and Dublin. The same helium, five years from now, will increasingly come from the Rukwa Basin. The sequence Jerez documents for lithium is not a prediction about Rukwa. It is a description of the pattern Rukwa is entering.

## Why this is not the cobalt story with a different mineral

Two things make the Rukwa case genuinely new, and both complicate the direct transfer of the cobalt or lithium analyses.

First, the timeline has compressed. The cobalt supply chain reorganization around electric vehicles took roughly a decade to develop, and the consultation failures documented in the Democratic Republic of Congo accumulated over that period. The AI buildout has compressed this timeline by roughly half. NVIDIA has forecast more than $1 trillion in revenue opportunity through 2027 for its Blackwell and Rubin chip families. Hyperscaler capital expenditure in 2026 alone is running at levels that the renewable energy transition took most of the 2010s to reach. The consultation infrastructure, state capacity in host countries, international legal frameworks, community organizing, has not had time to develop around the new demand vector.

Second, the distributional politics travel further than in previous cases. The electric vehicle story at least carries a climate argument that offers some moral purchase for the communities bearing extraction costs. The AI story offers no such argument at the point of extraction. A farmer in Momba District whose water table shifts as a result of helium development will not be told that her sacrifice contributes to reducing global emissions. She is contributing to the compute capacity of American and Chinese large language models. The value proposition is harder to make and the distributional justification is thinner.

This is where the welfare state literature becomes relevant in a way that AI infrastructure discourse has not yet grappled with. Koch and Fritz, writing in *European Political Science* (2023), argue that the pathways of green transitions are closely interrelated with welfare state setups because welfare states structure the material and cultural conditions that determine who wins, who loses, and who resists. The argument travels to AI infrastructure, but it travels in a direction that complicates standard welfare state scholarship. The host countries for the next phase of AI-driven mineral extraction are, with few exceptions, not inside any of the welfare regime typologies the comparative political economy literature uses. Tanzania, the Solomon Islands, Bolivia, the DRC: these are states with limited welfare infrastructure, contested state capacity, and high dependence on extractive revenue for public goods provision. The distributional buffer that even the weakest OECD welfare state provides does not exist.

Koch and Fritz do not discuss AI. Neither does any welfare state scholar whose work I have been able to locate. This is a gap in the literature that the next decade will fill in real time, usually by writing retrospective analyses of patterns that were visible in advance.

## What this means for the discourse

The dominant frame in the AI-helium discussion, exemplified by Bazilian's recent *National Interest* argument, is that Washington should treat helium as a strategic industrial gas within semiconductor and defense-industrial planning. The argument is correct as far as it goes. It is also an argument that takes the question "strategic for whom?" as already answered.

The communities in Momba District are also making strategic decisions, under conditions of severe informational asymmetry, against a timeline set in Seoul and Hsinchu and Santa Clara. The Noble Helium-UDSM partnership is genuinely unusual in its commitment to local capacity building. The Helium One CSR spending is genuinely above the Tanzanian regulatory floor. Neither of these facts means that the consultation process in Rukwa is structurally different from the ones Flemmer and Schilling-Vacaflor documented for Bolivia and Peru, or that Ash documented for the Solomon Islands. They mean the early stages look better than the comparison cases. The empirical record on whether the early stages predict the later stages is not encouraging.

The question that AI infrastructure writers are not asking is not whether helium should be treated as strategic. It is who gets to define strategic, on what timeline, with what voice at the table. This is a political economy question with a substantial academic literature and almost no presence in the AI discourse.

The next decade will produce a great deal of primary helium development in places the AI industry has not previously had to think about. And bromine production in Jordan. And germanium refining in southwest China. And rare earth processing wherever it can be permitted. The supply chain that produces the AI economy has a bottom, and the bottom is on land that belongs to communities whose prior consultation rights are formally recognized in international law and routinely unfulfilled in practice. Whether this time is different is a question worth asking, and one that nobody will answer without first taking it seriously.
