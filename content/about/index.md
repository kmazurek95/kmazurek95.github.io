I'm a quantitative social scientist based in Chicago. I study the distributional politics of AI: how artificial intelligence reshapes labor markets, energy systems, and political institutions, and why institutional configurations determine who bears the cost. My background is in comparative political economy and computational methods. I hold an MSc in Social Science Research from the University of Amsterdam and a BA in International Studies and Economics from Macalester College.

## The question

One question runs through my work: when structural economic forces create winners and losers, what determines whether societies respond with solidarity or exclusion? I did not arrive at this question through AI. I arrived at it through inequality, welfare states, and political attitudes, and the evidence kept pushing me in the same direction. For the argument and the empirical foundation, see the [research program](/research-program/).

## How I got here

I came to research through an unusual path. After Macalester I moved to Nablus in the West Bank to teach humanities and English at a bilingual school. Two years of classroom work, conversational Arabic, and daily life inside the political dynamics I had previously studied abstractly. International studies trains you to think about how international structures shape lived experience. The West Bank made that principle concrete.

I brought that orientation to my MSc at UvA, where the quantitative toolkit — multilevel modeling, measurement design, computational text analysis — caught up to the questions I had been asking. My thesis built an NLP classification pipeline across 78,000 congressional documents and earned an 8.0/10. I rebuilt the entire pipeline after graduating because I knew the methodology could be pushed further. Alongside and after the MSc, I built political data infrastructure at BallotReady across 20+ states, writing ETL pipelines and validation scripts for ballot measures, redistricting, and election records. That work kept me close to the concrete institutional plumbing that the comparative political economy literature is ultimately about.

## Why I work on this from the inside

For the past two years I have worked inside the AI industry doing measurement work: evaluation rubrics, benchmark design, annotation at scale, quality assurance. This is not a detour from the research. It is applied construct validity. At Tundra Technical Solutions, on assignment at Meta, evaluating LLM outputs at scale has taught me how evaluation rubrics actually behave under load — where inter-rater reliability drops, where ambiguous edge cases cluster, how guidelines have to evolve to match model failure modes. At Snorkel AI, designing graduate-level benchmark questions was a measurement-design problem: how do you construct items that discriminate between surface pattern-matching and real reasoning. At Character.AI, moving dialogue approval rates from 60% to 95% came from treating pedagogy as measurement design, with real student misconception patterns as the construct. These are the same questions I study formally. That is what "distributional politics of AI" means to me as a research program, not an abstract framing.
