## The question

One question connects everything I have done: when structural economic forces create winners and losers, what determines whether societies respond with solidarity or exclusion?

I did not arrive at this question through AI. I arrived at it through inequality, welfare states, and political attitudes, and then the evidence pushed me toward AI as the structural force that will test these institutional configurations most severely in the coming decades. The empirical foundation is four completed projects that all point in the same direction. My neighborhood analysis found that local inequality explains only 3.4% of redistribution attitude variance in the Netherlands. A cross-national extension using the European Social Survey found 7.7% at the country level, with a significant income-by-inequality interaction (p = 0.002). My county-level election model put 31% of partisan swing variance at the state level. Across different countries, different scales, and different data, one result is consistent: institutional context outperforms individual and local conditions.

That finding is what motivated the turn toward AI. If how societies absorb structural economic shocks depends on their institutional configurations, then the question for AI is not whether it will disrupt. It is which institutions will mediate the disruption, through what mechanisms, and for whom. AI does not create one kind of loser. It creates different kinds of losers through different channels: displaced workers, households absorbing energy costs from data center expansion, populations dependent on mineral supply chains they never chose. These channels interact. They often land on the same populations. Neither techno-optimism nor techno-pessimism captures this. The question is which institutions, protecting whom, under what conditions.

## How I got here

I came to research through an unusual path. After graduating from Macalester in 2018 with a concentration in Middle East Studies and a minor in Economics, I moved to Nablus in the West Bank to teach at a bilingual school. I spent two years there, teaching 26 classes a week in humanities and English, developing conversational Arabic, and learning what it means to live inside the political dynamics I had previously only studied in classrooms. International studies trains you to think about how international structures shape lived experience. The West Bank made that abstract principle concrete.

When I came back and started my research master's at UvA, I brought that orientation with me. My coursework in Advanced Multivariate Modelling, Measurement Models, Big Data and Automated Content Analysis, and Experimentation in the Social Sciences gave me the quantitative toolkit. My thesis gave me the chance to apply it: measuring interest group prominence in congressional floor speeches. That meant building a pipeline from scratch. 78,000 documents from the GovInfo API. A coding scheme I designed and iteratively refined. A supervised classifier that I pushed from F1 0.79 to 0.91. Multilevel regression models identifying what drives prominence across policy domains and partisan contexts. The thesis earned an 8.0/10, and I rebuilt the entire pipeline after graduating because I knew the methodology could be pushed further.

## The insider position

What happened after the master's is what makes my perspective on AI unusual. I did not go straight into a PhD. Instead, I spent two years inside the AI industry, doing the work that makes these models function: data labeling, benchmark design, evaluation rubrics, and quality assurance at scale.

At Meta (through Tundra Technical Solutions), I evaluate AI-generated responses across their LLM product suite, 450+ items weekly, maintaining a 95/100 quality score. The work requires applying structured evaluation criteria to edge cases that resist easy categorization: misinformation, AI-generated evidence images, culturally sensitive content. I have contributed to standardizing evaluation guidelines, improving inter-rater reliability across a team of 15, and documenting systematic model failure patterns that inform training data priorities.

At Snorkel AI, I wrote 150 graduate-level social science benchmark questions designed to expose gaps in model reasoning, with a roughly 90% acceptance rate, and reviewed over 1,000 peer-authored questions for rubric alignment and difficulty calibration. At Character.AI, I built economics tutoring dialogues and learned that technical accuracy without pedagogical grounding teaches nobody anything. I researched real student misconception patterns and redesigned my process, pushing approval rates from about 60% to over 95%. At Mercor and RWS Group, I designed scoring rubrics, failure taxonomies, and quality criteria for content evaluation.

This work is not a detour from the research. It is part of the research. I know what precarious AI labor looks like because I have done it. I know how evaluation rubrics fail because I have watched them fail, and sometimes built better ones. I have seen the gap between how the AI industry measures improvement and what that improvement actually means for the humans on both sides of the pipeline, the workers building the training data and the users consuming the outputs. That experience grounds my research in something most political economists writing about AI do not have: direct, sustained contact with the production process.

It also shapes a set of questions I care about beyond distributional politics. How do you actually evaluate whether a language model has gotten better, and better at what? How do you design measurement instruments that capture what matters rather than what is easy to count? How do you balance the drive to improve AI capability with the question of whether that improvement serves humans or replaces the work they do? These are measurement methodology questions, and they connect directly to the construct validity and inter-rater reliability challenges I studied in my MSc. My article ["What Political Science Can Teach AI Evaluation"](/posts/political-science-ai-evaluation/) makes this connection explicit.

## Before all of that

Before the AI work, I spent three and a half years at BallotReady building political datasets across 20+ states and thousands of jurisdictions. Elections, redistricting, officeholder records, from structured state databases to Excel files on county websites that had not been updated since the last cycle. I wrote ETL pipelines, ran validation checks, called county clerks to resolve discrepancies, and delivered analysis-ready datasets under campaign-cycle deadlines. I built a validation script after catching my own data error that reduced the team's error rate from 8% to under 2% and became part of standard onboarding. The political data infrastructure I built at BallotReady is the same kind of institutional plumbing that determines whether structural economic disruption is absorbed or amplified in different political systems.

## What I am working toward

I am pursuing PhD positions in computational social science and comparative political economy, with a focus on how AI-driven structural change interacts with institutional configurations to produce distributional outcomes. I placed in the top 7 of 184 applicants for the REPAIR PhD at VU Amsterdam, which studied welfare states and AI disruption. My primary current target is the ROOTS position at UvA, studying attitude-mediated social tipping dynamics. The bridge: my ESS finding that redistribution preferences drift gradually under inequality shocks while institutional trust tips through a self-reinforcing feedback loop connects directly to the mechanistic modeling of social tipping.

In parallel, I am applying to policy research roles where comparative political economy and quantitative methods are the lead, and to AI evaluation roles where measurement methodology and domain expertise position me to design evaluation frameworks rather than just execute them.

I have working papers in development that extend the institutional-context finding from my empirical projects into a theoretical framework for AI's distributional consequences across OECD countries. They are not yet public. When they are ready, they will appear on this site.
