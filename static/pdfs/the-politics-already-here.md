# The politics already here

The Iran war dominates the foreign policy conversation, and it should. But the domestic political consequences of the AI economy that the war has made visible are not waiting for the war to end. The coalition that will eventually set the terms of US AI regulation is forming in state legislatures and county zoning boards right now, and the people writing about AI policy in Washington are, in large part, still looking the other way.

This post is about what that coalition actually looks like, what it is asking for, and where the gap is between the regulatory architecture being built by default and the one the economics would actually warrant.

## The coalition

In the first four months of 2026, the AI industry has lost or delayed a growing list of data center projects. Virginia, which hosts the world's largest concentration of data centers, has turned. A Washington Post-Schar School poll conducted in late March found that only 35 percent of Virginia voters are comfortable with new data center construction in their community (*Washington Post*, April 15, 2026). On April 14, Prince William County abandoned plans for a 1,700-acre data center campus that would have been among the largest in the country. Maine legislators passed a measure this month blocking new data centers drawing more than 20 megawatts of power until fall 2027. That is the first state-level moratorium of its kind. It will not be the last.

The Data Center Watch project counted at least 48 projects blocked or delayed across the country in 2025, scuttling $156 billion in planned development (*Washington Post*, April 2026). State legislatures introduced 238 bills to restrict data center development; 40 passed. The pace is accelerating in 2026.

The character of the coalition is the part the industry's political strategy has not absorbed. Rallies this weekend in six Michigan cities pair Democratic state senators with Republican state representatives on the same stage. A Public Policy Polling survey found that 81 percent of Michiganders somewhat or strongly support a ballot initiative to ban utility political donations, with support distributed across the partisan divide (Planet Detroit, April 2026). In Wisconsin, Francesca Hong, a democratic socialist former restaurant owner, is running for governor on an explicitly anti-data-center platform (TIME, February 2026). In Virginia, state senator Danica Roem, a progressive Democrat, is one of the lead organizers. In Florida, Governor DeSantis has positioned data center restriction inside a property-rights frame. Brendan Steinhauser, a GOP strategist and head of the Alliance for Secure AI, has warned that politicians who choose to do the bidding of Big Tech at the expense of hardworking Americans will pay a significant political price (TIME, February 2026).

This is not a left-right fight. It is a local-cost versus national-competitiveness fight. The industry's political spending, which Silicon Valley leaders have publicly committed in the hundreds of millions for 2026 midterms, is calibrated for a partisan contest. The contest is not partisan.

## The labor question, missing

The labor displacement question, which the Citrini Research scenario introduced in February and which Citadel Securities has publicly rebutted, is being conducted in investor newsletters and economics journals. It has not yet produced visible political mobilization. Goldman Sachs's January 2026 outlook flagged that companies are increasingly discussing layoffs and are eager to use AI to reduce labor costs, and warned that an increased reluctance to hire in anticipation of AI being able to replace workers in the near future would represent a new obstacle to maintaining full employment (Goldman Sachs, January 2026). February payrolls showed a loss of 92,000 jobs. Unemployment is expected to reach 4.6 percent by year-end. None of this has, so far, produced the kind of organized political response that the energy cost channel has produced.

The mechanism that would translate it into politics exists. Voeten (2024), writing in *Comparative Political Studies*, demonstrated that energy cost increases produce measurable shifts toward radical-right voting through an economic grievance channel, not an ideological one. The same mechanism applies to AI-driven labor market pressure. The difference is attribution. A rate case tied to a specific data center campus is legible to affected voters. A hiring freeze at a regional employer who is "evaluating AI for customer service" is not, at least not yet.

The fusion point is coming. A household absorbing a higher electricity bill because a hyperscale data center was built thirty miles away, and that has a member facing a shrinking hiring pipeline at a regional employer who has publicly said they are deploying AI to reduce headcount, will not analytically decompose those two experiences. They will be processed as a single grievance. The Iran war acceleration of energy costs has brought that fusion closer by removing the lag that would otherwise have separated the two channels.

When the fusion arrives, the labor-side regulatory question will enter state politics the same way the energy-side question did. It will not be filtered through federal labor policy. It will arrive through zoning boards, utility commissions, and state ballot initiatives, and it will arrive in language that defense and tech industry lobbyists have not rehearsed against.

## What voters are asking for

The policy demands on the energy side are now well-defined. Cost allocation reform to make data center customers pay for their own grid upgrades rather than socializing the cost through base rates. Moratoriums, typically one to two years, on new large-load interconnections pending environmental and rate impact studies. Tax break repeals, particularly for operators whose promised local economic benefits consist of 20 to 50 permanent jobs against multi-hundred-megawatt loads. Utility political donation bans, where the argument is that monopoly utilities should not fund the campaigns of the regulators who approve their rate cases. The Michigan ballot initiative is the clearest example of this line of attack.

What is missing from the demand stack is any serious labor-side policy architecture. There are no state-level AI displacement tax proposals in serious consideration. There are no portable benefits models tied to AI-related workforce disruption. There is no proposal, left or right, to condition tech industry tax incentives on verified labor retention. The policy imagination on the labor side is where the policy imagination on the energy side was in 2022. The vocabulary is still being developed.

This is the gap that will determine whether the bipartisan coalition currently organizing around data centers expands into a broader political realignment on AI, or whether it remains a siting-and-rates movement that the industry can contain through state-level accommodations. Coalitions that stay narrow lose. Coalitions that broaden win policy change. The question is who articulates the labor frame first, and in what language.

## The architecture being built by default

Federal AI policy in 2026 is thin. The Biden-era executive order was rolled back. Congressional action has been limited to narrow bills on deepfakes and election content. The regulatory architecture being built is happening at the state level, in patchwork form, without coordination. This is a predictable outcome of federal inaction combined with state-level political pressure, and it is a poor outcome on its own terms.

Patchwork regulation creates compliance overhead without producing coherent policy. Data center operators will comply with the strictest state regime in their service territory and then lobby for federal preemption. Labor-side regulation, when it arrives, will face the same fragmentation. The question for AI policy is not whether there will be regulation. There will. The question is whether there will be a federal architecture that addresses the compounding distributional dynamics the energy-plus-labor channel is producing, or whether the federal government will cede the terrain to the states and eventually step in to preempt them on industry-friendly terms.

The political economy of that choice is not obscure. Federal preemption of state AI regulation, if it arrives, will be the defining tech-policy fight of the next Congress. The coalition currently forming around data centers is the one that will prosecute it, assuming the coalition remains intact when the fight moves from zoning boards to Capitol Hill. Whether it remains intact depends on whether it finds a labor frame in the interval.

## The war made the costs visible

The Iran war did not create the AI economy's distributional problem. The problem was structurally present before the war. What the war did was remove the lag that typically separates energy shocks from political mobilization. Brent at $95 a barrel in April, gasoline above $4 a gallon, helium and bromine constraints on the semiconductor supply chain, and the measurable shift toward inland US data center siting that the Gulf risk has already produced: these all arrived together, in a compressed window, alongside visible local data center disputes that were already underway. The compression is what created the opening for the coalition.

The foreign policy conversation will move on. The ceasefire will hold, or will not. The Strait of Hormuz will reopen, or will remain contested. The helium supply will restart, or will be replaced by North American and African primary production over the next three to five years. All of these are important. None of them resolve the domestic political question.

The AI economy has entered American politics. It entered through the electricity bill. The coalition that will determine how it is regulated is forming in state legislatures and county zoning boards right now. The foreign policy crowding-out effect is real, and temporary. The political economy underneath is durable, and it is the terrain on which the next round of this argument will be fought.
